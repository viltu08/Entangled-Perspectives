How do you balance the complexity of the narrative at hand with the abstraction to create emotional or experiential impact on the viewer?

"The balance comes with the assignment in hand. Personally, emotional response comes from your unique vision as an artist/designer to the problem in hand, so at the end, my Venn diagram is unique in comparison to the Venn diagram of other person."

Difference between “complex” and “complicated”

I used the words ‘complex’ and ‘complicated’ interchangeably, but that changed when I started looking at the projects I have done and writing about what parts I find complicated and what I find complex in the project.

I realised it was a complicated process when I started learning to use HTML and CSS for Web design in 2024. There are multiple rules and syntaxes to learn. But, once you get used to those, you can apply similar methods to create various effects. The process started becoming uncomplicated by sequencing the code neatly and logically.

While the languages used to build a website are complicated, designing an interactive website about Monsoon Memories is complex. I could create a controlled layout and apply all the CSS rules for perfect animations, but the complexity arises from user interaction. I could not predict which window someone would interact with first or if they would prefer poems to be more like the typographic playgrounds of existing poems. One interaction leads to another and creates a domino effect of unexpected experiences, which varies from individual to individual.

So, developing a fully functional, beautiful site is a complicated experience for me, but adapting and iterating the same site based on user experience is a complex process.

Something complicated seems resolvable once I follow a given set of rules or logic. Still, a complex system requires constant work and responsiveness, as all its elements behave symbiotically and continuously alter how we experience them.

A complex system like Monsoon Memories exists within a Web of influences. I observed that people don’t just interact with the site; they bring their memories, contexts, and moods into the experience. This mutuality means the site isn’t just a fixed-designed object—it’s a living, breathing and altering system shaped by the user and the maker. The website has changed exponentially since its conception because of these observations.

When people share what they find engaging or confusing on the site, it creates a loop where their actions influence my design choices, and my design choices reshape their experience, which calls for continuous adaptation in design.

By putting in enough time and effort, I can achieve some form of predictability for a complicated design, but a complex design keeps changing, almost like a living organism.

In that sense, Crafting Complexity isn’t about finding solutions for complex problems but designing spaces focusing on interactions and being flexible for evolution of those ideas.

Dr. Martin Lorenz

What is the difference between a complex visual system and a complicated visual system for you?

"Okay, so, I think complexity is natural, the world is complex, but the function of design is to make the complex less complicated. There's this discussion about simplicity and complexity and the conclusions that I reached are that design should allow, or should build a simple bridge to complexity because we shouldn't simplify reality, but we need to make information accessible. I'm seeing it as a process, we have to allow simple bridges or pathways towards a more complex understanding of things in general."

Shifting Perspectives, Complexity as a Spectrum

Looking at stars at night brings me joy and boosts my curiosity. At first glance, they are simple twinkling dots of light—small, distant, and seemingly straightforward. But if we shift our perspective and look through a telescope, those dots transform. Suddenly, we start seeing colours, textures, and variations. They’re no longer just glowing points in the sky. With advanced telescopes, we can determine the elements they’re made of, the time of their birth and death, the radiations they emit, and intricate structural details. This shift in perspective adds layers to our understanding, providing more context to visualise those stars in their surrounding environment—a cosmos filled with galaxies, nebulas, and worlds unseen.

Here, complexity becomes a choice, not a necessity. It’s something we can dial up or down, depending on the narrative we wish to create. This is an objective view of complexity—one rooted in data and observation. But what happens when the perspective shifts to something more subjective? What if I start describing the emotions I feel when I look at those stars? Does that change how the stars exist? It doesn’t. Instead, it opens up a new layer—a possibility of what the stars could mean to different sentient beings.

In my book design project, Conscious Cosmos, I explore how I can transfer the control over complexity to the reader. The primary text from Robert Lanza’s Biocentrism offers a revolutionary view of the universe by asking us to view it from a lens of consciousness and biology, in addition to physics, chemistry and mathematics. He uses a mix of storytelling and facts to make the theories accessible to the reader. The secondary text, generated using ChatGPT (artificial consciousness) evolves into orbiting electrons—haikus, songs, and metaphors—that eventually dominate the pages. The modular nature of this layout allows the reader to toggle between different levels of complexity. Much like exploring the stardust using a telescope, the reader decides how immersed they would like to be in the content.

When I approach a project, I dive deeply into research. You could say I always have a telescopic approach to exploring a new topic. I don’t always know what the final outcome will look like. Will I end up creating a complex website to share the narrative, or will I simplify everything into a single poster that symbolizes the idea? It’s during the process of designing that I make choices—revealing and layering the information I’ve gathered.

Complexity feels inherent to the way I work, think, and write. My brain energizes like a multiverse-traveling spaceship, imagining hundreds of ways to approach a single topic. Sometimes I choose structure to create clarity and accessibility. For instance, in my project The Measure of Victory, I create ten plates using a data visualization approach to dissect everything I know about the Trajan Column. Here, complexity lies in the layered narrative, ranging from the material composition to the history and emotions tied to its existence. Yet, the final output is modular—simple, interconnected modules that form a richer story when viewed together.

In contrast, when I want to express the Visual Tactility of the Trajan Column, I design a video-motion project. Using microscopes, I record textures and project them onto paper sculptures inspired by natural patterns. This transforms my microscopic observations into an immersive, macroscopic experience. Here, complexity emerges through layered interpretations, visual metaphors, and maximalist renderings that blend sound and sight. The plates serve as a direct visualisation of the research, while the motion graphics open a space for interpretation and emotional connection.

Fact and Fiction—Complexity as a mix of reality and imagination

A combination of real and imaginary numbers, complex numbers make rendered motion possible in digital design.

z = a + bi

z = complex number
a = real number | in design, grid
b = real number | in design, modules
i = imaginary number | abstraction

Here, complexity is achieved by mixing reality and imagination. For such a perspective, the reality consists of physically tactile entities, and the imagination provokes visual tactility. The only way to achieve complete understanding is to embrace such complexity in the way we think. It’s the same way a poet/poetess creates fantastical worlds for us, which otherwise wouldn’t be possible to imagine. In a way, the concept of complexity as a mix of reality and imagination is equivalent to creating immersive experiences that mix real physical entities with imaginary ideas.

I explore this concept through the project, Quantum Type Lab, a digital gallery for experimental typography where I overlap design and scientific principles, exploring intersections of atomic structures and the transformative potential of variable typefaces. This project treats typography as a mutable entity—one that, like atoms, can exist in different states: ground, excited, or ionized. Typography here is not static; it mutates, transforming from one form to another and embodying the fluidity of both waves and particles as in quantum theory.

As with complex numbers, the combination of real and imaginary elements within the typography system brings forth new forms and interactive possibilities. It is a space where typography is both real (rooted in physical design of glyphs, grids, and modules) and imaginary (expanding into abstract forms based on imagined quantum behavior of typography). The pulsating portals allow you to travel between glyphs, and the particle-based punctuations energize on hovering. This immersive experience allows users to engage with typography that abstracts scientific principles through interactive visualizations.

“I collect things from reality and find an interesting or weird point in it. Then I tweak the content a bit—these tweaks come from imagination. For example, in my Artificial Intelligence project 'Remove Nipples', I started with a real image and blended more images to create a new thing. I didn’t abandon the real thing; I used it as a source. For the'Too Cute to Be True' zine, I took real examples and added more imagination to make the final piece. Imagination is not a default setting. It’s like a filter or an action you put into reality to create something new. I need rules to start with, so I find them in reality.

We both merge reality and imagination, but you start with imagination and anchor it in reality to build complexity. I start with real concepts and add imagination to create ‘magic.’”

Dr. Martin Lorenz

I came across your mention of the Norm Studio in FVS book and I ended up on their website and saw their Numerical Designs with the diagrammatic representation of motion, which led me to write about complex numbers. I'm a big fan of metaphors, so I use complex numbers as a metaphor because it's a mix of real numbers and imaginary numbers to get a better understanding. In design, I see grids and modules as reality and the abstraction we achieve out of them as the imagination part. I wonder, do you think such mathematical and scientific concepts can support an argument for the necessity of complexity in design?

"Yeah, for sure. But I think one of the difficulties that we are facing at the moment is that we do not have one language anymore to understand reality or we understood that reality can't be understood just through one perspective, and that we need to somehow establish frameworks that allow us to access reality through different perspectives. We have to live with multi-perspective visions of reality. But I think it should be clear that it is one of the perspectives and that we need different contrasting languages. There's an interesting small table in the beginning of the book by Jacques Batin about the kind of information that can be accessed through what kind of visual language and he's talking about abstract and concrete language as well. We can access more metaphorical or meta understandings of reality through something very abstract. But then we need as well a more specific and concrete representation of reality through numbers and statistics. I find this interesting that we do not have to choose one or the other, but we should use them together to get a multi-perspective view on reality or whatever we are trying to display or research."

Multi-modal and Multi-sensorial

To be written by Vishakha.

Amira Hanafi

In A Dictionary of the Revolution, you used definitions to document protest language that carries wildly different meanings to different groups. How did you design that rigid format to hold space for contested interpretations while still making a coherent work? I’d love to hear how this applies to projects wiith structured frameworks like CreaTures Glossary where you have a multi-modal approach.

"I think that one of the most important aspects of my work on A Dictionary of the Revolution was that it was long and sustained. I thought of the project idea while I was participating in the first weeks of the uprising in Egypt in 2011. I didn't start to work on the project until 2014, so I had already had three years to live in the context of the revolution and think about how language was functioning in that space. I think that without this lived experience it would have been really impossible for me to organize the project in the way that I did.

I'm not sure what you have in mind when you say "rigid format" because there were actually several systems of organization that I used within the project. One was the vocabulary box which was organized into four categories. Another was the way that I found interlocutors for the project, by assembling a team of interviewers and using a snowball interviewing method. Once I had recorded hundreds of hours of conversation, I used another system to listen to the recordings and create an audio archive. In each recording, a person would speak about multiple terms drawn from the vocabulary box. Rather than listen to one person's recording at a time, I would listen to all of the recordings for a single term, which meant I was cycling through different voices every day. This heightened the sense of complexity of meaning and informed the final design of the work, which is circular and non-linear.

The digital publication and the diagrams were the final organizing aspect of the project. They came after I had already composed texts for each of the 125 terms in the book. I decided how to organize this material for an online reader at a point where I had a very deep understanding of the material I had worked with. I had developed a very intimate relationship to the people and the voices in the project, which informed how I wrote the texts. One of the researchers I had worked with, Ahmed Refaat, had suggested to me early in the project that we might organize the terms into families. This is how I came to decide on the relational aspect of the digital work. I wanted to answer the question, "What do people speak about when they speak about 'x' term?" I worked with a programmer, Youssef Faltas, who presented me with some options for how we might visually represent the relationships I wanted to establish.

Yes, CreaTures Glossary approaches meaning-making in a different way, but it's actually closely tied to the work I did on A Dictionary of the Revolution. You can see from how I described my work on A Dictionary that it was multi-modal and was engaged with language as a social practice. For CreaTures Glossary, I wanted to focus more on the fluid, relational aspect of language and meaning-making that I explored in the research phase of A Dictionary of the Revolution, using the vocabulary box. But CreaTures Glossary does this in a digital space as opposed to the live conversations that I held for the research in Egypt. CreaTures Glossary was also very experimental. I built a number of different components that work together, but now I am working on each of those components separately to really investigate their potential. Conceptually, I am interested in building digital tools that facilitate meaning-making in relation to the people that are using them.

Advantage of multi-lingual complexity

To be written by Vishakha.

Dr. Martin Lorenz

How would you imagine a flexible visual system catering to multiple languages for an identity? How could we achieve that variation while still keeping a constant or cohesive aesthetic? If there is a system which is catering to a group of people who are bilingual, for example, and we use language within the system. It might be Hindi and English and they might have very different aesthetic, how languages and the glyphs look. So how would we work in a system where multiple languages are there and still have a cohesive aesthetic going on?

"That's another very interesting question. I was thinking a lot about this because, in the digital world, it becomes easier and easier to be responsive to users using their identities. I'm not sure if this is the right way to go, because otherwise, we are again in this affirmative algorithms that are frictionless and don't make you grow. They just repeat what you're already thinking. I think that actually it is fine if people would be confronted with ideas that are different from their own ideas because this gives design the opportunity to play a role in culture and collective learning and visual literacy. So, what might be interesting is if you want to think about design as a cultural voice, then how can design give context about what it is doing and why it is doing what it does? Just to give transparency about the motives and being transparent about where this is coming from and why it is done. Of course, in commercial design, nobody wants this because they just want to persuade people to do what they want. But, if you would see design as a part of culture or something that is building culture or constructing culture, then this transparency would be definitely something that I would find interesting. Something that maybe I am not the target audience of would become interesting because I would get the chance and the opportunity to learn about a different culture or a different perspective. So, I would be happy to get something that is maybe done for an audience in Latin America because it would give me a chance to learn about their visual codes and cultural codes."

Systems

To be written by Vishakha.

Dr. Martin Lorenz

I was thinking of different sciences and I've been reading this book, Let's Become Fungal.It's basically mycelial teachings by Yasmine Ostendorf and it has documentation of indigenous people and women identifying people from Latin America, and their knowledge. When I was reading about the mycelial networks in the book, it made me think about your approach towards systems as well. It was very interesting for me to think that, could you imagine mycelial or rhizomatic systems as flexible visual systems or any unconventional structures like fluid or fractal-based systems?

"Yeah, that's very interesting. it's nice. I like that you have this in my writings. But I don't know if I was aware of this when I wrote the book. I'm more and more aware of this as well of the way how I think about systems now, that they're hard to plan. So they have to organically grow and emerge. I definitely think that there's a relationship between this kind of fungal neural networks of forests and systems. I think mainly the distinctions that I would make is that we have to be very conscious about the difference between artificial and living systems. We as people, we are living systems and we might not even see everything that connects us and how we are connected. But when we're designing a system that is most of the times an artificial system. So we might be more in the planning state and not that much in the emergence state. I came to this realization that very artificial systems are more likely to break because they don't correspond to the reality of an organization. For example, like the typical thing that a planned system is not going to be used by the people because it doesn't correspond to their way of working. This is something that I came to realize that when we design systems, they're intended to be flexible but sometimes they're not, because it's an artificial system and it's something that we planned . We might not have seen everything that we need to apply this system or integrate and implement it in the organization that has to use it."

Yeah, it makes me think of letting go of control at some point in systems.

"Yeah, that's interesting. It's because when you're designing, you're trying to control a situation that you might not be able to foresee because you're designing for something that is going to happen in the future. You're designing something for people that are not with you when you design it. So how can you design a system that can flexibly change as well in the future and be more emergent and evolutive in the process? From personal experience I think, and it's not just me who says this, like Interbrand talks about the brand cycle. I think it's a problem that we as designers get commissioned and then we leave the project. Actually, we should be part of the project and be able to be there when it's changing and adapt. I think that's one of the critical problems."

I wonder if a flexible visual system that represents an organization changes based on how the organization is evolving. For example, if the organization introduces new policies to be inclusive, the FVS mirrors that transformation and I feel like you're talking about something similar.

"Exactly. If we talk about flexible systems as identity systems, I think then we have to talk as well about different functions of it, because one is the identification. From the outside, what is this organization identified for or recognized by? The other thing is, internally. Do we have a purpose as an organization that we all can stand behind? This can be very abstract, something in the future. But then you have the actual day-to-day work, and in the book that I have written Flexible Visual Systems, this circle is about corporate action, corporate image. It's the day-to-day communication. Does this represent as well what we want to achieve as an organization? So, I think it's a continuous interaction in between these points. What is our identity? What do we want to achieve long term? What's our purpose? How are we seen from the outside and how we're actually behaving and communicating is reflected?"

Accessibility

To be written by Vishakha.

Dr. Martin Lorenz

How do you think one can use complexity to provide more clarity and accessibility instead of chaos in design?

"Well, the image that comes to my head is the classes that I'm giving in Barcelona and here in Germany. We do a lot of topic(??) mappings, and we always come to the point where the maps become too complex for an outsider. So the insider who did the map still understands or knows how to navigate through the map. It's like what Dana Meadows [Donella (Dana) H. Meadows] says, a model is a model, and it's not reality, but it helps us to get a more real perspective on reality, and then we can make this more accessible through design."

Scientific approach and rigor

To be written by Vishakha.

Dr. Martin Lorenz

There was another person I wanted to bring up because I've been reading a lot recently, and she has been a great inspiration to me. Her name is Rebecca Elson. She was a Canadian-American astronomer and writer who discovered dark matter. It's been funny that in the last two weeks, I've come across the phrase "dark matter" so many times. The organization you work for, Dark Matter LabsRebecca Elson discovered dark matter, I'm taking a class on surveillance media, and I read a chapter called "Racialized Surveillance," and it was a book called "Dark Matters" by Simone Brown. Apple TV is throwing at me a TV show now called "Dark Matters" to watch. I've been thinking about "Dark Matters" a lot, and what I like about Rebecca Elson is that she was a scientist, but her work often blends data with emotional resonance. She used to write poetry and describe complex mysteries of the scientific concepts. In flexible visual systems, I was thinking, how do you balance the logical, data-driven aspects of design with the emotional or experiential impact? Can it be both mathematically precise and emotionally engaging?

"That's a very deep question. I think the easiest way for me to answer this is to answer as a human. I think we can feel math or physics because we all are born on this planet. We experience the same physical laws and are able to communicate with each other. In my thinking or in my designing, I don't think much about math or physical laws. I'm trying to intuitively feel what I'm doing there because math and physics are embedded already in this feeling. I think that this kind of intuitive way of working and thinking comes closest to how the user of the system will perceive the identity as well. So it's for me, it's a very intuitive process. What maybe is a little bit more mathematical is later, the description of the system when you are making a design manual, and you try to be very clear about the rules. When I'm designing, I don't really think about the rules. Then, in retrospect, I have to think, why did I do this? So, how can you put this into a phrase or a rule so that someone else understands what you were doing there and can reproduce it in a similar way? I think this is when the objectivity of math comes more into play. That's why I'm very careful about automation of design processes because at the end, it's still communication by or in between humans and if it feels wrong, then it's probably wrong. I think you have to access this, your gut feeling about it, to make a decision if something works or if it doesn't work."

The complexity of preserving indigenous crafts through visual systems

To be written by Vishakha.

Dr. Martin Lorenz

Do you think that flexible visual systems principles can help democratize design tools, maybe for non-experts or communities trying to preserve their craft traditions visually? Do you think democratizing those tools or the way of thinking could help?

"Yeah, I think it definitely could help, but I think it shouldn't be the systems that I propose, because I think every culture has it's own visual systems. Just look at patterns, you find patterns everywhere over the world in every century. You can go back and you can travel wherever you want to. I wouldn't like this kind of democratization that happens through digital tools because they just make everything look the same. I would be more happy if flexible visual systems would be seen as an approach, and then each person could access their own systems. I'm trying to just give not even a template, but a methodology of how you could work and think. But then you should access your own creativity and your own systems because otherwise, we will have this big mish-mash that's a bit boring. I like that people bring in their own systemic thinking."

Dr. Martin Lorenz

I have an undergrad in Textile design before I studying Graphic design. I've grown up with patterns and motifs around me. While reading the flexible visual systems, there's this part of me whenever I try to work with visual culture of India, as I'm from India, I keep thinking about the preservation of indigenous crafts and motifs. I always get in this area where I cannot decide, if I apply digital systems or digital thinking which can help with those preservation or if that's not the way to go. I was wondering, what do you think about that and preservation of such visual traditions using digital systems?

"I think for me it's the most interesting thing to do. We had this experience when we did a project with Two Points for Jordania, and Lupi traveled to Jordan and Aman. She saw everywhere that there were food trucks with a flexible system on them. It was always a pattern, similar colors and similar grids, but always different kinds of patterns. We used this then as an identity for architects from Aman. But we also experienced that they were telling us, the cool new stuff is like the commercial language of the States, which we find quite boring, and we like more the traditional patterns. But they say they are perceived as old-fashioned and maybe too traditional. So, I understand that you don't want to be conservative about it, but it would be nice if it could be a fresh new young interpretation of what is there, because otherwise, we end up with everything looking like it's in design templates."

Dr. Martin Lorenz

It's making me think of this designer, if you know her, Vanessa Zúñiga Tinizaray. She's from Ecuador and she uses Processing software and works with indigenous motifs and crafts. She did a Zoom presentation at RISD a couple of years back, and just with one single object, she made thousands of systems out of it. I was wondering that seems very interesting - you're giving a different life to an existing motif and pattern.

"Yeah, this is exactly what I was meaning. There's so much discovery in history and everywhere. She took something from her own culture but I wouldn't mind if someone is discovering something from a different culture. It's nice to have a little bit more diversity in the visual systems. I don't care if someone from the States would look at something French or something German or... I think it would be sad if all this cultural richness would be lost. So I don't care who does it, but it would be nice if people would do what she did, like picking this up and seeing how this could be transported into today's life and possibilities."

Dr. Martin Lorenz

Nice. Yeah, because when I thought of her work in relation to this thought, I was thinking ornamentation means different things to me than to a person in America, a person in Germany. So, for you, how do you define structure and ornamentation? Do you think both terms can work together in a system? If we are doing this cultural preservation using it, my definition of ornamentation might be different than any other culture. How do I make sure I'm not appropriating it?

"Yeah, see, this is the thing that maybe sounds a bit provocative, but I actually don't care. Of course, I care if a big fashion company just steals something from people and then they make a lot of money with something that they did not create or invent. Of course, I'm bothered by this. But I'm also bothered by big AI companies just stealing the work of illustrators and designers and making money off it. This is the same kind of botheredness I have about it. But when you look at the design history of Germany, then I find it ridiculous to talk about Swiss design or German design or Russian design, because we wouldn't have had the Bauhaus if there wouldn't have been the Russian constructivist. So is Bauhaus Russian or is it German? Then you wouldn't have the Swiss design. If the German designers and artists wouldn't have to flee Nazi Germany and go to Switzerland or you wouldn't have the kind of Paul Rand and Saul Bass designers if they wouldn't have opened up Bauhaus in schools in America. So if you just go back long enough, then you see that it's all a big mix of things. I think it's a bit sad to just pin it down to different people or pin it down to different countries because borders, they're just inventions, they actually don't exist. Also the stockhold(??) about people doesn't exist. I think the Bauhaus just existed, not because they're geniuses. It's like Brian Eno, he's talking about the Scenius that makes geniuses. So the Bauhaus wouldn't have been in the middle of the Netherlands where they had the De Stijl and the Russians. Then a knowledge-flow wouldn't have existed. I think the knowledge flow is much more important than the actual people that we are looking at. The same about the Renaissance, if there wouldn't have been the Silk Road, then they wouldn't have gotten all this knowledge from math, from the Arabs and the knowledge from the Orient. There was the Italian Renaissance. It sounds for me absurd because they were just in at the right time, at the right place, where all the knowledge went through. It might sound a little bit provocative, but this culture appropriation thing is a bit weird to me. I think the stock hold (??) is a bit weird to me. I hope you understand what I'm trying to say."
